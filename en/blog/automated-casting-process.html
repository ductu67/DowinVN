The Automated Sand Casting Process — From Sand to Finished Casting

📅 20/02/2025 ⏱️ 10 min read ✍️ DOWIN Engineering

Sand casting is one of the oldest metal casting methods — but modern automation technology has transformed it into a precise, repeatable and highly efficient process. This article explains each step in the fully automated closed-loop casting line that DOWIN designs and installs.

Overview: Why a closed loop?

In traditional manual sand casting, each step is carried out by a separate group of workers. Mold quality depends on the skill and consistency of each individual. A fully automated closed-loop line eliminates that dependency — every step is controlled by pre-set PLC parameters, ensuring that mold number 500 at the end of the day has the same quality as mold number one.

The 6 Steps of the Line

01
Sand Preparation

The automated sand mixer blends three components: silica sand (60–70%), bentonite (binder, 8–12%) and water (3–4%). A continuous moisture sensor monitors the mix and the system automatically adjusts the water addition to reach the target moisture level (typically 3.2–3.8%).

The mix meets specification when it simultaneously satisfies: green compressive strength ≥ 80 kPa, gas permeability ≥ 100, and surface hardness 85–90 HSB. Sand that fails is returned to the mixer for re-blending automatically.

02
Molding

This is the central step of the line. The DW molding machine receives sand from a conveyor or hopper, fills the flask, then presses at 0.6–1.0 MPa (depending on model). The pattern — a positive replica of the casting — is pressed into the sand to form the cavity.

Each complete mold consists of two halves: the cope (top) and the drag (bottom). Cycle time to produce a complete mold set: 15–25 seconds.

03
Core Setting

Cores create internal voids in the casting — for example, the bore of a pipe or the internal chambers of an engine housing. Cores are made separately from self-setting sand.

Depending on line configuration, this step may be fully automated (robotic core placement) or semi-automated (operator places cores guided by the control system). After core setting, the two mold halves are assembled and clamped together.

04
Metal Pouring

Molten metal (1,350–1,500°C for cast iron) is poured into the mold through the gating system. Automated lines typically use a temperature-controlled pouring furnace with automatic flow control, or a tilting pouring ladle with weight monitoring.

Precise control of the poured metal volume is critical to quality — under-filling causes incomplete castings, over-filling wastes metal and makes shake-out harder.

05
Cooling

Poured molds are transferred to the cooling conveyor. Cooling time depends on the wall thickness of the casting — from a few minutes to over an hour for heavy sections.

Cooling too fast causes thermal cracking; too slow wastes time and affects the metal's microstructure. The conveyor speed is set to the specific parameters of the casting being produced.

06
Shakeout & Sand Recovery

The vibratory shakeout machine breaks apart the sand mold, separating the casting from the sand. The casting moves on to cleaning and finishing operations.

The sand is recovered through a screening, cooling and testing system. Sand meeting specification returns to the mixer for reuse — typically 70–80% of sand is recycled. Sand that has become saturated with bentonite is discarded and replaced with fresh sand.

Benefits compared to manual casting

  • Output: 120–250 molds/hour vs. 20–40 molds/hour manually
  • Labour: Reduced by 60–70%, from 10–15 people to 3–5 people
  • Quality: Mold defect rate reduced from 5–8% to 1–2%
  • Safety: Less direct contact with molten metal and sand dust
  • Sand saving: 70–80% recycled vs. 100% discarded in manual casting
