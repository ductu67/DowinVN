5 Key Factors When Choosing a Green Sand Molding Machine

📅 15/03/2025 ⏱️ 8 min read ✍️ DOWIN Engineering

In 15 years of advising hundreds of foundries across Vietnam, we have observed that most poor machine purchase decisions share a common flaw: choosing the lowest price while ignoring long-term operating factors.

This article summarises the 5 questions a DOWIN engineer always asks a foundry owner before recommending any model.

1
Mold size — not output rate

Most foundry owners immediately ask "how many molds per hour?" — but the more important question is "what is the largest mold size I need to produce?"

A machine producing 180 molds/hour with a 1,200×1,000mm mold is useless if your casting requires a 1,400×1,100mm mold. Equally, buying a 1,600mm machine for small products wastes floor space and capital.

The practical rule: the machine's mold size should be at least 20% larger than your largest casting to allow space for the gating system and risers.

Tip: List the 5–10 casting types you produce most frequently, measure the largest dimensions, then choose a machine with a mold 20–30% larger than that figure.

2
5-year energy costs — more important than purchase price

A traditional hydraulic machine runs its motor continuously at full power regardless of whether the machine is in its press cycle or waiting. A servo machine only draws power according to actual load. The difference can be 35–40% in energy consumption.

For a foundry running 2 shifts per day, 300 days per year, energy savings from a servo machine can offset the purchase price premium in 18–24 months.

Calculation: (kWh/mold difference) × (molds/year) × (energy price) = savings/year. Compare this against the purchase price difference to find the payback period.

3
Spare parts availability and local service support

This is the most frequently overlooked factor. Some low-cost machines imported from emerging markets have no official dealer in Vietnam. When a machine breaks down, you must order parts from overseas and wait 2–6 weeks while the line is stopped.

Questions to ask every supplier:

  • Where is the spare parts warehouse? Is stock held in Vietnam?
  • What is the typical delivery time for common parts?
  • In which cities are your repair technicians based?
  • Can you provide a reference list of current customers?
4
Future line expansion capability

Many foundries start with a standalone machine but after 2–3 years want to add an automatic sand mixer, conveyor or automatic pouring system. If the machine has no industrial I/O or connectivity, you will have to replace the machine rather than upgrade it.

Even if you have no automation plans right now, choosing a machine with an open PLC and standard I/O ports is a low-cost investment that keeps future options open.

5
Output headroom — don't buy "just enough"

A machine running at 90–95% capacity continuously will wear out faster and break down more often than one running at 70–75% capacity. It will also have no room to handle order spikes.

The practical rule: Choose a machine with 25–30% more capacity than your current requirement. If you need 130 molds/hour, choose a machine rated for 160–180 molds/hour. The price difference is usually under 15%, but service life and reliability improve significantly.

Summary: Pre-contract checklist

  • ✓ Mold size suits your largest casting (+ 20% headroom)
  • ✓ 5-year energy costs calculated, not just purchase price
  • ✓ Supplier has spare parts stock and technicians in Vietnam
  • ✓ Machine has standard PLC, supports future line integration
  • ✓ Machine output is at least 25% above current requirement
